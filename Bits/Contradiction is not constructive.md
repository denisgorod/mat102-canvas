The proof about digits of $\pi$ shows that *some* digit occurs infinitely often, but it doesn't tell you *which* one. This is typical of contradiction proofs: they demonstrate that something must be true without revealing the specific case. In fact, whether every digit appears infinitely often in $\pi$'s decimal expansion is an open problem — nobody knows the answer.

A contradiction proof is **existential without being constructive**. It says "something exists" but doesn't hand you an example. By contrast, if you wanted to prove "there exists a digit that appears infinitely often" by construction, you'd need to point to a specific digit and show it appears infinitely often — much harder.

This gap between existence and construction becomes important as mathematics grows more abstract. Some statements can *only* be proved by contradiction, while others have constructive proofs that are more illuminating.

>[!idea]
> Contradiction proofs establish that something *must* be true, but may not reveal *what* it is.
