Every mathematical result has two parts: the hypothesis (the condition that must first be true) and the conclusion (what is guaranteed if the hypothesis holds).

Consider this example: "If $a$ and $b$ are even integers, then $ab$ is even." Here, the hypothesis is "$a$ and $b$ are even integers" and the conclusion is "$ab$ is even." The word "if" signals where the hypothesis begins; the word "then" points to the conclusion. The power of the result lies in the logical connection: whenever the hypothesis is satisfied, the conclusion *must* follow.

Why does this distinction matter? Because before you apply a mathematical result, you must first ask: "Does my situation satisfy the hypothesis?" A theorem only guarantees its conclusion when its hypothesis is met. If your values of $a$ and $b$ are not both even, you cannot conclude that $ab$ is even using this result — the theorem simply does not apply.

This is more subtle than it first appears. Not all mathematical statements are phrased with "if...then." Sometimes the hypothesis is implied. For example, "The sum of the angles in a triangle is 180°" silently assumes you are working with Euclidean geometry, not some other geometric system. Learning to identify hypotheses and conclusions — explicit or hidden — is essential to reading mathematics correctly.

>[!idea]
> A mathematical result consists of a hypothesis (the condition required) and a conclusion (what is guaranteed); you can only apply the result if the hypothesis holds.
