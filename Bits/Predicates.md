Consider $P(x)$: "$x$ has green hair." You can't determine whether $P(x)$ is true without specifying a value for $x$. Here $P$ is a **predicate** and $x$ is the variable. Substituting a value turns a predicate into a proposition: $P(\text{your mother})$ is the proposition "Your mother has green hair."

Similarly, "$x^2 > \pi$" is a predicate — its truth depends on $x$. But "$2 + 2 = 5$" is already a proposition (a false one), because there are no unspecified variables. The key distinction: a proposition has a definite truth value; a predicate needs you to plug something in first.

>[!idea]
> A predicate is a statement with free variables; plugging in values converts it to a proposition.
