When you raise an element to a power and generate a cyclic subgroup, the subgroup you get depends on the gcd of the exponent and the element's order.

**Concrete example:** In $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, let $a = 1$ (which has order 12). Consider $\langle 1^4 \rangle = \langle 4 \rangle$. What elements are in this subgroup? Since $\gcd(4, 12) = 4$, the formula predicts $\langle 4 \rangle = \langle 1^{\gcd(4,12)} \rangle = \langle 1 \rangle$ (the subgroup generated by 1). Wait, that doesn't match. Let me reconsider: $\langle 1^4 \rangle$ means the cyclic subgroup generated by $1^4 = 4$. The elements are $\{0, 4, 8\}$ (multiples of 4 mod 12), which has order 3. And $\langle 1^{\gcd(4,12)} \rangle = \langle 1^4 \rangle = \langle 4 \rangle$, so actually they're the same subgroup. Good.

**General statement:** If $a$ has order $n$, then $\langle a^k \rangle = \langle a^{\gcd(k,n)} \rangle$.

Prove this by showing two-way inclusion. First show $\langle a^k \rangle \subseteq \langle a^{d} \rangle$ where $d = \gcd(k, n)$ (use Bézout's identity). Then show $\langle a^d \rangle \subseteq \langle a^k \rangle$.

>[!question] Exercise
> Let $G$ be a group, $a \in G$ with $|a| = n$, and $k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. Show that $\langle a^k \rangle = \langle a^{\gcd(k,n)} \rangle$.
