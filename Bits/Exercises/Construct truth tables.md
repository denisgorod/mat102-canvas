Building truth tables for compound statements is a systematic way to understand what they mean. Start by identifying all the atomic propositions, list all possible combinations of their truth values, then work column by column from the simplest subexpressions to the most complex.

>[!question] Exercise
> Construct truth tables for the following statements:
> 1. $(P \wedge Q) \vee R$
> 2. $(P \wedge \neg Q) \vee (Q \wedge \neg R)$
> 3. $(P \vee R) \wedge (Q \vee R)$
> 4. $\neg(P \vee Q) \vee (Q \wedge \neg R)$
