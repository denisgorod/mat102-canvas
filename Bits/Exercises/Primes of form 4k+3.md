# Infinitely many primes of the form 4k+3

**Part (a).** By imitating Euclid's proof of the infinitude of primes, show that there are infinitely many prime numbers of the form $4k + 3$ where $k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.

Hint: Suppose there are only finitely many such primes. Consider the number $N = 4(p_1 p_2 \cdots p_m) + 3$, where $p_1, \ldots, p_m$ are all primes of the form $4k+3$. Show that $N$ is not divisible by any of them and cannot be prime, leading to a contradiction.

**Part (b).** Explain why the same argument does **not** work for primes of the form $4k + 1$. Where does the proof break down?

>[!question] Exercise
>Note that $(4a+1)(4b+1) = 4(\cdots) + 1$, but $(4a+3)(4b+3) = 4(\cdots) + 1$. How does this affect the form of $N$?
