You've learned that in a group, every element has an inverse, and you can raise elements to powers. But what happens when you combine these two ideas?

Take a specific case first. In the group $(\mathbb{Z}_7^*, \times)$, let $a = 2$. Compute $(2^{-1})^3$ directly (find the inverse of 2, then cube it). Then compute $(2^3)^{-1}$ (cube 2 first, then find the inverse). Do you get the same answer?

Now prove that $(a^{-1})^n = (a^n)^{-1}$ in any group, for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$. (Hint: what is $(a^n) \cdot (a^{-1})^n$?)

>[!question] Exercise
> Prove that $(a^{-1})^n = (a^n)^{-1}$ for all $a$ in a group $G$ and all $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$.
