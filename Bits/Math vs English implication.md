Most if-then sentences in English are secretly iff. If your mother says "If you do the dishes, then you can go out tonight," there's an implicit understanding that *not* doing the dishes means *not* going out. Let $D$ = "you do the dishes" and $O$ = "you go out." She said $D \Longrightarrow O$, but you also understand $\neg D \Longrightarrow \neg O$ (the inverse, equivalent to $O \Longrightarrow D$). So she really means $D \Longleftrightarrow O$, even though "you will do the dishes if and only if you go out" sounds strange.

Also, English implications are typically *causal* — the hypothesis explains the conclusion. Mathematical implications need not be. "If it's raining, then an asteroid will hit Jupiter" is a perfectly valid mathematical statement — it can be true even without a causal link.

>[!idea]
> English implications usually hide an iff and assume causality; mathematics uses implications purely for logical dependency, without causality.
