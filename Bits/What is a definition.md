In English, a definition is often a helpful approximation. "A chair is something you sit on" — true enough for most purposes, but not airtight. A mathematician would say: that is not a definition, it is a rough description.

A mathematical definition is a *complete, precise prescription* for what the word means. No intuition. No wiggle room. The definition is all there is.

Here is an example:

>[!d] Definition
> An integer $n$ is **even** if there exists another integer $m$ such that $n = 2m$.

That is the complete truth of evenness. Nothing more, nothing less. Either $n = 2m$ for some integer $m$, or it is not even. Do not rely on what "even" feels like to you.

There are other ways to phrase this same idea: "$n$ is a multiple of $2$", or "$n/2$ is an integer", or "$n$ leaves remainder $0$ when divided by $2$. All of these are equivalent. But saying "$n$ ends in $0, 2, 4, 6,$ or $8$" is not a mathematical definition — it works in base 10 but falls apart if you change the number system. A true definition works everywhere.

When you encounter a definition in a textbook, you are reading the complete specification of what the word means *for the rest of the course*. Your job is to understand it so fully that you can work with it, even in situations the definition does not explicitly mention.

>[!idea]
> A mathematical definition is a precise, complete statement with no room for intuition. It is the entire specification of what a word means in that context.
