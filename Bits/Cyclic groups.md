You've been working with powers of a single element. What if you take all powers of one element — do they form a subgroup?

**Definition:** Let $g \in G$. The **cyclic subgroup generated by $g$** is
$$\langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}.$$

The group $G$ is called **cyclic** if $G = \langle g \rangle$ for some $g \in G$ (i.e., $G$ consists of all powers of a single generator).

**Examples:**

1. $(\mathbb{Z}_4, +) = \langle [1] \rangle = \{[0], [1], [2], [3]\}$. We have $[1]^1 = [1]$, $[1]^2 = [2]$, $[1]^3 = [3]$, $[1]^4 = [0]$. Also, $\langle [3] \rangle = \langle -[1] \rangle = \{[0], [3], [2], [1]\} = \mathbb{Z}_4$. So $[1]$ and $[3]$ are both generators. A cyclic group may have multiple generators.

2. $(\mathbb{Z}_4, +)$ also has the subgroup $H = \langle [2] \rangle = \{[0], [2]\}$, which is a proper subgroup.

3. $(\mathbb{Z}, +) = \langle 1 \rangle$ is cyclic with generator 1. Also, $\langle -1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}$ (the negative integers plus zero).

4. Not every group is cyclic. For example, $S_3$ (the symmetric group on 3 elements) has 6 elements, but no single element generates all of them. If $\sigma$ is a transposition, then $\langle \sigma \rangle = \{e, \sigma\}$ has only 2 elements.

**Notation:** In additive groups, we write $\langle g \rangle = \{ng : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. For instance, $\langle 3 \rangle$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ is the set of all multiples of 3.

Cyclic groups are the simplest and most structured groups. Once you understand cyclic groups, you can use them as building blocks to understand more complex ones.

>[!idea]
> A cyclic group is generated by a single element $g$: $G = \langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ — every element is a power of $g$.
