You have just read a new definition. What do you do next?

Do not sit passively. Do not move on to the next paragraph hoping it will make sense later. Instead, start hunting for examples.

As soon as you read the definition, immediately try to generate examples and non-examples. Sample widely: positive integers, negative integers, zero, large numbers, small numbers, special numbers. Do not just pick the first thing that works — deliberately look for variety.

Why? Because exploring examples builds intuition that is *grounded in the definition itself*. When you test "$0$ even" or "$-4$ even", you are not relying on vague feeling — you are applying the precise rule and seeing what happens. That process teaches you what the definition *really means* in practice.

Here is the rhythm:

1. Read the definition carefully.
2. Find three examples that satisfy it.
3. Find at least one non-example that does *not* satisfy it.
4. Try an edge case (often zero, negative numbers, or extremes).
5. Try to explain to yourself *why* each example belongs where it does.

When you do this actively, the definition stops being a abstract phrase and becomes a tool in your hands.

>[!idea]
Right after reading a definition, deliberately hunt for examples and non-examples across a diverse range of cases. This builds intuition grounded in the definition itself, not vague feeling.
