Unlike other academic fields, mathematics is unique in that mathematical results, if proven correct, are never wrong, have no counterexamples, and are not subject to interpretation. The proposition "if $a$ and $b$ are both even, then $ab$ is even" is true — always, no matter what. This absolute certainty is what makes mathematics special.

Think about other disciplines for a moment. In physics, we refine our theories as we gather new evidence. In psychology, researchers debate the reliability and interpretation of studies. In history, scholars reinterpret events as new documents emerge. But in mathematics, once a result is proven, it is settled. A mathematical truth is not an opinion, a consensus, or a provisional model. It is a logical necessity.

This does not mean mathematicians never make mistakes — they do. But when someone claims "I have proven that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational," they are claiming something very specific: that there is a chain of reasoning, starting from accepted axioms, that makes this conclusion unavoidable. If that chain is sound, the result is eternal. No new data will overturn it. No reinterpretation will change it.

>[!idea]
Mathematical theorems, once proven, are infallible and unchanging — this certainty is what distinguishes mathematics from empirical sciences.
