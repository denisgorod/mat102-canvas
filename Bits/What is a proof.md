Given that mathematical theorems are infallible, how do we prove something to absolute certainty? A proof is a series of statements which begin with the hypothesis, end with the conclusion, and are such that the truth of any line follows logically from the previous statements. As long as all statements are objectively true, the proof establishes the theorem.

Think of a proof like a chain: each link must be solid, and each link must connect to the next. You start at the hypothesis — this is your foundation. Then, step by step, you make claims that follow logically from what you already know. At each stage, the reasoning must be transparent: "Because this is true, then that must be true." You continue until you reach the conclusion. If every step is sound, no one can dispute the result.

What makes a proof different from just asserting something? Assertion says "I believe this is true." A proof says "Here is why this *must* be true, given the hypothesis." The audience may not believe your assertion, but they cannot argue with a logically sound proof — assuming they grant the axioms you start with.

Proofs can be long or short, straightforward or intricate. A simple proof might take five lines; a complex one might span pages. But regardless of length, the structure is the same: start with something you know (the hypothesis), and build toward the conclusion using only valid logical steps.
