Fix an element $g \in G$. We've defined $\langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. Is this a subgroup of $G$?

**Proposition:** For any $g \in G$, $\langle g \rangle \le G$.

*Proof:* We use the subgroup test. First, $\langle g \rangle$ is nonempty since $e = g^0 \in \langle g \rangle$.

Now let $a, b \in \langle g \rangle$. Then $a = g^k$ and $b = g^\ell$ for some integers $k, \ell$. We need to show $ab^{-1} \in \langle g \rangle$.

We have
$$ab^{-1} = g^k \cdot (g^\ell)^{-1} = g^k \cdot g^{-\ell} = g^{k - \ell}.$$

Since $k - \ell$ is an integer, $g^{k-\ell} \in \langle g \rangle$.

By the subgroup test, $\langle g \rangle \le G$.

**Why this matters:** Every cyclic subgroup generated by a single element is automatically a subgroup. So when you compute all powers of some $g$, you get a subgroup "for free." The closure and inverse properties come automatically from the definition $\{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$.

**Consequence:** If $G = \langle g \rangle$ is cyclic, then every subgroup of $G$ is also cyclic (generated by some power of $g$). This is a key structural fact: cyclic groups are very rigid, and all their subgroups inherit that cyclic structure.

The simplicity of the proof reflects the power of the subgroup test. One algebraic identity ($g^k \cdot g^{-\ell} = g^{k-\ell}$) is enough to verify that $\langle g \rangle$ is a subgroup.

>[!idea]
> For any element $g \in G$, the cyclic subgroup $\langle g \rangle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is a subgroup of $G$ — the exponent arithmetic $g^k \cdot g^{-\ell} = g^{k-\ell}$ ensures closure and inverses.
