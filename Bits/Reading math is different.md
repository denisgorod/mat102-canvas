When you read a novel, you start on page 1 and move forward. When you read a math textbook, you bounce. Experts skip around constantly — they'll spot a term they don't recognize, jump backward to find its definition, then leap forward to see an example, then backtrack again to check a lemma. It's not a flaw in how they read; it's how mathematics *must* be read.

Eye-tracking studies show the difference starkly. A student reading math will stare at a formula, trying to decode the symbols. An expert reading the same passage focuses on the surrounding words and explanation, glancing at the formula only to confirm what the text already said. The expert knows that the exposition — the narrative glue — is where the thinking happens. The formula is just a compressed checkpoint.

Learning to read mathematics is not something that happens automatically once you know the symbols. It is a skill you can practice and improve. The first part of that skill is understanding what different kinds of blocks do in a math text — what a definition is, what a theorem does, how they fit together — and then building the habit of using them actively rather than passively scanning.

This changes how you should read. It is not enough to let your eyes move across the page. You need to be prepared to loop backward, to test ideas, to ask questions of what you read.

>[!idea]
Mathematics is read non-linearly, with constant backtracking to definitions, examples, and supporting results. This is how experts read; learning to do it is the core skill.
