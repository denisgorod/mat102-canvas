>[!d] Definition
> The **converse** of $P \Longrightarrow Q$ is the statement $Q \Longrightarrow P$.

The converse of a true statement is **not necessarily true**. Consider: "All dogs are animals" ($D(x) \Longrightarrow A(x)$) is true. But "All animals are dogs" ($A(x) \Longrightarrow D(x)$) is false — a cat is an animal that is not a dog. Never assume the converse holds without a separate argument.

>[!idea]
> The converse of $P \Longrightarrow Q$ is $Q \Longrightarrow P$, but a true implication does not guarantee its converse is true.
