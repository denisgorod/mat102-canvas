Consider a regular $n$-gon (an equilateral triangle, square, pentagon, etc.) placed in the plane. A **symmetry** of the $n$-gon is a rigid motion (rotation or reflection) that maps the polygon to itself.

The set of all symmetries of a regular $n$-gon, under composition, forms a group called the **dihedral group** $D_n$.

Let's work out $D_3$, the symmetries of an equilateral triangle. Label the vertices 1, 2, 3 in order. There are two types of symmetries:
- **Rotations:** A rotation by $120°$ clockwise (call it $r$), and a rotation by $240°$ (which is $r \circ r = r^2$). The identity rotation $e$ (by $0°$) completes the set $\{e, r, r^2\}$.
- **Reflections:** Each reflection is through a line from a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite edge. There are 3 reflections. Call one of them $s$.

So $D_3$ has 6 elements: $\{e, r, r^2, s, sr, sr^2\}$.

Here's the key: every element of $D_3$ can be written as a product of $r$ and $s$. We don't need to list all the reflections separately — they're all generated by $s$ combined with $r$.

**The group operation is composition:** $(a \circ b)$ means "first apply $b$, then apply $a$."

**$D_3$ is non-abelian:** The relation $sr \neq rs$ fails commutativity. To see this concretely: $r$ rotates the triangle $120°$ clockwise, while $s$ reflects through a fixed axis. Apply them in different orders and the triangle ends up in different positions.

In fact, there's a famous relation in $D_3$: $sr = r^2 s$. This encodes the interaction between rotation and reflection and is central to understanding the group's structure.

For a general $n$-gon, $D_n$ has $2n$ elements: $n$ rotations and $n$ reflections. It is non-abelian for all $n \geq 3$.

>[!idea]
> The dihedral group $D_n$ is the group of symmetries of a regular $n$-gon under composition. For $D_3$, it has 6 elements $\{e, r, r^2, s, sr, sr^2\}$ where $r$ is a $120°$ rotation and $s$ is a reflection — and it is non-abelian because reflections and rotations don't commute.
