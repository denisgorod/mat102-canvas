Not all forms of active reading are equally helpful. It's easy to confuse self-explanation with two weaker strategies: paraphrasing and monitoring. All three *look* like you're engaging with the text, but they have very different effects on understanding.

**Paraphrasing** just restates in different words. You read "$a$, $b$, and $c$ are even integers" and write "$a$, $b$, and $c$ have to be positive or negative, even whole numbers." You've swapped vocabulary, but you haven't added any information. You haven't connected the line to the structure of the proof, to why those variables matter, or to how they relate to earlier steps. Paraphrasing can feel like you're working hard, but it doesn't deepen understanding.

**Monitoring** means noting that you've processed something. You read a line and think "OK, I understand that..." or "I see, so..." — you're describing your own thought process. You're aware of what you read, but you're not explaining *why* the line was necessary or how it contributes to the proof's logic. Monitoring shows effort, but it doesn't build connections.

**Self-explanation** is different. It connects the line to prior knowledge and the proof's structure. When you read "$2(k + \ell + m)$ is even," a good self-explanation is: "Because the sum of three integers is an integer, and 2 times an integer is even by definition." You've linked the new information (what we're claiming) to existing knowledge (the definition of even). You've explained *why* the claim holds.

The difference is in what you add. Paraphrasing adds vocabulary. Monitoring adds awareness. Self-explanation adds *logical connection*. When you explain each line in terms of concepts you already know, you build a mental scaffold that lets you understand not just *what* the proof says, but *how it works*.

As you practice, you'll develop a feel for it. The test is simple: if you can explain to a friend why each step was needed and how it connects to the whole argument, you've been self-explaining. If you've just restated the words or said "I read it," you haven't.

>[!idea]
> Self-explanation builds logical connections between new information and prior knowledge. Paraphrasing and monitoring are weaker strategies that restate or note understanding without adding these connections.
